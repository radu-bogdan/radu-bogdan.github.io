Maxwell stress tensor derivation

Notation

Let $\widehat\Omega\subseteq\mathbb{R}^3$ be a reference domain comprised of points denoted by $\widehat{x}\in\widehat{\Omega}$. Further, we define a placement mapping $\widehat{p}:\mathbb{R}^+\times \widehat{\Omega}\to \mathbb{R}^3$, $x:=x(s):= \widehat{p}(s,\widehat{x})$ and a sequence of domains

$$\begin{aligned} \Omega(s) = {x = \widehat{p}(s,\widehat{x}), \forall,\widehat{x}\in\widehat{\Omega}}\end{aligned}$$

We choose $\widehat{p}$ such that $\Omega(0)=\widehat{\Omega}$, i.e. $\widehat{x}= \widehat{p}(0,\widehat{x})$. The inverse of $\widehat{p}$ we denote by $p^{-1}:\mathbb{R}\times \Omega(s)\to \widehat{\Omega}$ via $\widehat{x}= p^{-1}(s,x)$. We also write $\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x}) = \widehat D_{\widehat{x}}\widehat{p}(s,\widehat{x})$ for the jacobian of $\widehat{p}$, aka the deformation gradient, as well as $\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) = \det \widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})$ for the functional determinant. Since $\widehat{p}$ is invertible at every point, by the chain rule/implicit function theorem, we have

$$\begin{aligned} D_x p^{-1}(s,x) = (\widehat{D}_{\widehat{x}} \widehat{p}(s,\widehat{x}))^{-1} = \widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1}\end{aligned}$$

Also, let $v(s,x) := \widehat{v}(s,\widehat{x}) := \partial_{s}\widehat{p}(s,\widehat{x})=x'(s)$. Then

$$\begin{aligned} D_x v(s,x) &= D_x (\widehat{v}(s,p^{-1}(s,x)) = \widehat{D}_{\widehat{x}} \widehat{v}(s,\widehat{x})D_x p^{-1}(s,x) \\ &= \widehat{D}_{\widehat{x}} \widehat{v}(s,\widehat{x})(\widehat{D}_{\widehat{x}} \widehat{p}(s,\widehat{x}))^{-1}= \partial_{s}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x}) \widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1}\quad\text{and} \\ \partial_{s}\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) &= \widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})\text{div}_x v(s,x) \end{aligned}$$

The second formula follows from Jacobi’s formula, see the appendix in the end. Also, for vectors $h$ and $b$, we have

$$\begin{aligned} h\cdot (D_x v)b = \sum_j h_j\sum_i b_i \partial_{i}v_j = (h\otimes b):D_xv = (b\otimes h):\nabla_x v\end{aligned}$$

Reynolds transport theorem for a standard density

Before we begin, let us briefly recall the proof of the Reynolds transport theorem. Let $\widehat\rho:\mathbb{R}^+\times\widehat{\Omega}\to\mathbb{R}$ be a given density and we let $\rho:\mathbb{R}^+\times\Omega(s)\to\mathbb{R}$ with

$$\begin{aligned} \rho(s,x):=\frac1{\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})}\widehat\rho(s,\widehat{x})\end{aligned}$$

Then the derivative satisfies

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}\widehat\rho(s,\widehat{x}) &= \frac{d}{ds}\Big(\rho(s,x)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})\Big) =\frac{d}{ds}\rho(s,x)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) + \rho(s,x)\partial_{s}\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) \\ &= \Big(\partial_{s}\rho(s,x) + \nabla_x \rho(s,x)\cdot x'(s) + \rho(s,x)\cdot \text{div}_x v(s,x) \Big)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) \\ &= \Big(\partial_{s}\rho(s,x) + \nabla_x \rho(s,x)\cdot v(s,x) + \rho(s,x)\cdot \text{div}_x v(s,x) \Big)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})\end{aligned}$$

by employing the product and chain rule. Then

$$\begin{aligned} &\frac{d}{ds}\int_{\Omega(s)}\rho(s,x),dx = \int_{\widehat{\Omega}}\frac{d}{ds}\widehat\rho(s,\widehat{x}),d\widehat{x}\\ &=\int_{\widehat{\Omega}}\Big(\partial_{s}\rho(s,x) + \nabla_x \rho(s,x)\cdot v(s,x) + \rho(s,x)\cdot \text{div}_x v(s,x) \Big)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}),d\widehat{x}\\ &=\int_{\Omega(s)}\partial_{s}\rho(s,x) + \nabla_x \rho(s,x)\cdot v(s,x) + \rho(s,x)\cdot \text{div}_x v(s,x),dx\end{aligned}$$

or without arguments in compact form:

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}\int_{\Omega(s)}\rho = \int_{\Omega(s)}\partial_{s}\rho + \nabla_x\rho\cdot v + \rho\text{div}_x v\end{aligned}$$

which is the known result. Easy, right?

Energy densities in magnetostatics

Unlike the energy density $\rho$ defined above, the energy densities for the magnetostatic problem depend additionally on the $a$ and $b$ fields. We use red color to emphasize the additional dependencies in the following. Let $\widehat\rho:\textcolor{red}{\mathbb{R}^3}\times\mathbb{R}^+\times\widehat{\Omega}\to\mathbb{R}$ on the reference configuration be given. We then define the displaced energy density $\rho:\textcolor{red}{\mathbb{R}^3}\times \mathbb{R}^+\times \Omega(s) \to \mathbb{R}$ by

$$\begin{aligned} \rho(\textcolor{red}{b},s,x):= \frac{1}{\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})}\widehat\rho\left(\textcolor{red}{\frac{1}{\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x}),\widehat{b}},s,\widehat{x}\right)\end{aligned}$$

In a similar fashion, we define $\eta:\textcolor{red}{\mathbb{R}^3}\times \mathbb{R}^+\times \Omega(s) \to \mathbb{R}$ and $\widehat\eta:\textcolor{red}{\mathbb{R}^3}\times \mathbb{R}^+\times\widehat{\Omega}\to\mathbb{R}$ by

$$\begin{aligned} \eta(\textcolor{red}{a},s,x):= \frac{1}{\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})}\widehat\eta\left(\textcolor{red}{\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{-\top}\widehat{a}},s,\widehat{x}\right)\end{aligned}$$

Piola transformations and implications

Let $\widehat{b}:\mathbb{R}^+\times\widehat{\Omega}\to\mathbb{R}^3$ and $\widehat{a}:\mathbb{R}^+\times\widehat{\Omega}\to\mathbb{R}$ be given vector and scalar fields on the reference configuration, respectively. We then define $b:\mathbb{R}^+\times \Omega(s) \to \mathbb{R}^3$ and $a:\mathbb{R}^+\times \Omega(s) \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying

$$\begin{aligned} b(s,x) := \frac{1}{\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})\widehat{b}(s,\widehat{x}) \qquad\text{and}\qquad a(s,x) := \widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{-\top} \widehat{a}(s,\widehat{x})\end{aligned}$$

which follows from the corresponding Piola transformations for $H(\text{div})$ and $H(\text{curl})$. In particular, this implies that the derivatives satisfy the following:

$$\begin{aligned} &\frac{d}{ds}b(s,x) = \frac{d}{ds}\left(\frac1{\widehat{J}(s,\hat x)}\widehat{F}(s,x)\widehat{b}(s,\widehat{x})\right)\\ &=\partial_{s}(\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1})\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})\widehat{b}(s,\widehat{x}) + \widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1}\partial_{s}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})\widehat{b}(s,\widehat{x}) + \widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1} \widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})\partial_{s}\widehat{b}(s,\widehat{x})\\ &=(1)+(2)+(3)\end{aligned}$$

where

$$\begin{aligned} (1) &=-\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1}\partial_{s}\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) \widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1} \widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})\widehat{b}(s,\widehat{x}) = - b(s,x)\text{div}_x v(s,x) \\ (2) & = \widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1}\partial_{s}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})\widehat{b}(s,\widehat{x}) = D_x v(s,x) b(s,x)\\ (3) &= \partial_{s}b(s,x)\end{aligned}$$

from which we infer that

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}b(s,x) = -b(s,x)\text{div}_x v(s,x) + D_x v(s,x) b(s,x) + \partial_{s}b(s,x)\end{aligned}$$

Similarly, we derive

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}a(s,x) &= \frac{d}{ds}\left(\widehat{F}(s,x)^{-\top}\widehat{a}(s,\widehat{x})\right) \\ &= \partial_{s}(\widehat{F}(s,x)^{-\top})\widehat{a}(s,\widehat{x}) + \widehat{F}(s,x)^{-\top}\partial_{s}\widehat{a}(s,\widehat{x})\\ &=-\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{-\top}\partial_{s}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{\top} \widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{-\top}\widehat{a}(s,\widehat{x}) + \widehat{F}(s,x)^{-\top}\partial_{s}\widehat{a}(s,\widehat{x}) \\ &= -D_x v(s,x)^\top a(s,x) + \partial_{s}a(s,x)\end{aligned}$$

Differentiating the energy densities

Again, we use red to emphasize the additional terms we get in comparison to the standard transport theorem. We have

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}\widehat\rho\Big(&\textcolor{red}{\frac{1}{\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x}),\widehat{b}(s,\widehat{x})},s,\widehat{x}\Big)= \frac{d}{ds}\Big(\rho(\textcolor{red}{b(s,x)},s,x)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})\Big) \\ &=\frac{d}{ds}\rho(\textcolor{red}{b(s,x)},s,x)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) + \rho(\textcolor{red}{b(s,x)},s,x)\partial_{s}\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})\\ &= \Big(\textcolor{red}{\partial_{b}\rho\cdot\frac{d}{ds}b(s,x(s))} + \partial_{s}\rho + \nabla_x \rho\cdot v + \rho\cdot \text{div}_x v \Big)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) = (\star)\cdot\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})\end{aligned}$$

where we see that we only get one additional term to differentiate. Plugging in the term we derived in the previous section gives

$$\begin{aligned} (\star) &= \textcolor{red}{\partial_{b}\rho\cdot(-b\text{div}_x v + (D_xv) b + \partial_{s}b)} + \partial_{s}\rho + \nabla_x \rho\cdot v + \rho\cdot \text{div}_x v \\ &=(\rho -\textcolor{red}{\partial_{b}\rho\cdot b})\mathbb{I}:\nabla_x v + \textcolor{red}{\partial_{b}\rho\cdot(D_xv) b} + \textcolor{red}{\partial_{b}\rho\cdot\partial_{s}b} + \partial_{s}\rho + \nabla_x \rho\cdot v \\ &=\Big((\rho -\textcolor{red}{\partial_{b}\rho\cdot b})\mathbb{I} + \textcolor{red}{b\otimes \partial_{b}\rho} \Big):\nabla_x v + \textcolor{red}{\partial_{b}\rho\cdot\partial_{s}b} + \partial_{s}\rho + \nabla_x \rho\cdot v\end{aligned}$$

Similarly, we get

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}\widehat\eta\left(\textcolor{red}{\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{-\top}\widehat{a}},s,\widehat{x}\right) &=\Big(\textcolor{red}{\partial_{a}\eta\cdot\frac{d}{ds}a(s,x(s))} + \partial_{s}\eta + \nabla_x \eta\cdot v + \eta\cdot \text{div}_x v \Big)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) = (\dagger)\cdot \widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})\end{aligned}$$

where

$$\begin{aligned} (\dagger) &= \Big(\eta\mathbb{I} - \textcolor{red}{\partial_{a}\eta\otimes a} \Big):\nabla_x v + \textcolor{red}{\partial_{a}\eta\cdot\partial_{s}a} + \partial_{s}\eta + \nabla_x \eta\cdot v\end{aligned}$$

Energy and its minimizers

From the above densities, we define the energy functional

$$\begin{aligned} E(a,s) = \int_{\Omega(s)}\rho(b(s,x),s,x),dx-\int_{\Omega(s)}\eta(a(s,x),s,x),dx := E_\rho(b,s) - E_\eta(a,s)\end{aligned}$$

where $b(s,x)=\text{curl}_x a(s,x)$. For any fixed $s = s^*$, we choose

$$\begin{aligned} a^* = \text{argmin}_{a}E(a,s^*)\qquad\text{with}\qquad b^*=\text{curl}a^*\end{aligned}$$

Then, for the directional derivative

$$\begin{aligned} \nabla_w^a E(a,s):=\lim_\lambda\frac1\lambda(E(a+\lambda w,s)-E(a,s))\end{aligned}$$

we have that

$$\begin{aligned} 0 = \nabla_w^a E(a^*,s^*)= \int_{\Omega(s)}\partial_{b}\rho\cdot \text{curl}w-\partial_{a}\eta\cdot w\end{aligned}$$

where of course $\partial_{b}\rho=\partial_{b}\rho(b^*(s^*,x),s^*,x)$ and $\partial_{a}\eta=\partial_{a}\eta(a^*(s^*,x),s^*,x)$. Thus, $a^*$ is the solution to the corresponding variational problem. Later on, on the discrete level, $a^*_h$ will be our finite element solution.

Energy derivatives

Plugging in our optimum $a^*$ (and the derived $b^*$) and differentiating w.r.t. $s$ yields

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}E_\rho(b^*,s) &= \frac{d}{ds}\int_{\Omega(s)}\rho(b^*(s,x),s,x),dx \\ & = \int_{\widehat{\Omega}} \frac{d}{ds}\widehat\rho\left(\frac{1}{\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x}),\widehat{b}^*(s,\widehat{x}),s,\widehat{x}\right),d\widehat{x}= \int_{\widehat{\Omega}} (\star)\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}),d\widehat{x}\\ & = \int_{\Omega(s)} \Big((\rho -\partial_{b}\rho\cdot b^*)\mathbb{I} + b^*\otimes \partial_{b}\rho\Big):\nabla_x v + \partial_{b}\rho\cdot\partial_{s}b^* + \partial_{s}\rho + \nabla_x \rho\cdot v ,dx(s)\end{aligned}$$

Similarly, we obtain

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}E_\eta(a^*,s) &= \int_{\Omega(s)} \Big(\eta\mathbb{I} - \partial_{a}\eta\otimes a^* \Big):\nabla_x v + \partial_{a}\eta\cdot\partial_{s}a^* + \partial_{s}\eta + \nabla_x \eta\cdot v,dx(s)\end{aligned}$$

Altogether, we have

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}E(a^*,s) &= \int_{\Omega(s)}\Big(\rho - \partial_{b}\rho\cdot b^* - \eta\Big)\mathbb{I}:\nabla_x v(s,x) + \int_{\Omega(s)} (b^*\otimes \partial_{b}\rho + \partial_{a}\eta\otimes a^*):\nabla_x v(s,x) \\ &+\int_{\Omega(s)} \partial_{s}\rho -\partial_{s}\eta + \int_{\Omega(s)}(\nabla_x\rho-\nabla_x\eta)\cdot v +\int_{\Omega(s)} \partial_{b}\rho\cdot\partial_{s}b^* - \partial_{a}\eta\cdot\partial_{s}a^*\end{aligned}$$

The last term vanishes now since $a^*$ is the optimizer, see the previous section for $w:=a^*$.

Force and torque

In the following, $\widehat P$ denotes the power (Leistung), i.e. the force applied by the vector field $\widehat{v}$. By definition, we have

$$\begin{aligned} \widehat P(\widehat{v}):=\frac{d}{ds}E(a^*,s^*)\Big|_{s^*=0} &= \int_{\widehat{\Omega}}\Big(\widehat\rho - \partial_{b}\widehat\rho\cdot \widehat{b}^* - \widehat\eta\Big)\mathbb{I}:\nabla_{\widehat{x}} \widehat{v}(\widehat{x}),d\widehat{x}\\ &+ \int_{\widehat{\Omega}} (\widehat{b}^*\otimes \partial_{b}\widehat\rho + \partial_{a}\widehat\eta\otimes \widehat{a}^*):\nabla_{\widehat{x}} \widehat{v}(\widehat{x}),d\widehat{x}\\ &+\int_{\widehat{\Omega}} \Big(\partial_{s}\widehat\rho -\partial_{s}\widehat\eta\Big) ,d\widehat{x} +\int_{\Omega(s)}(\nabla_x\widehat\rho-\nabla_x\widehat\eta)\cdot v,d\widehat{x}\end{aligned}$$

where $\widehat{v}(\widehat{x})=\widehat{v}(0,\widehat{x})$, $\widehat\rho = \widehat\rho(\widehat{b}^*,\widehat{x}) = \rho(\widehat{b}^*(0,x),x,0)$, $\widehat\eta = \widehat\eta(\widehat{a}^*,\widehat{x}) = \eta(\widehat{a}^*(0,x),x,0)$, and so on. For computing the torque, we choose a special velocity field

$$\begin{aligned} \widehat{v}_\omega(\widehat{x}) := \begin{cases} \omega\times \widehat r(\widehat{x}) \text{ in the rotor} \\ (\omega\times \widehat r(\widehat{x}))\gamma \text{ in between}\\ 0\text{ in the stator} \end{cases} \end{aligned}$$

for a fixed angular velocity $\omega$ and a continuous function $\gamma$ that is $1$ on the rotor boundary and $0$ on the stator boundary. This ensures that $\nabla_{\widehat{x}} \widehat{v}(\widehat{x})$ exists, at least in a weak sense. Then the total torque $T$ is defined (implicitly) by

$$\begin{aligned} T\cdot \omega = \widehat P(\widehat{v}_\omega)\end{aligned}$$

Since both torque and angular velocity point in the same direction, we have that

$$\begin{aligned} |T| = |\omega|^{-1}\widehat P(\widehat{v}_\omega)\end{aligned}$$

which we use for computation.

Example

If the material law is linear, then

$$\begin{aligned} \widehat\rho(\widehat{b}^*,0,\widehat{x}) = \frac1{2\mu}|\widehat{b}^*|^2, \qquad \widehat\eta(\widehat{a}^*,0,\widehat{x}) = 0\end{aligned}$$

from which it follows that $\nabla_{\widehat{x}}\widehat\rho$, $\partial_{s}\widehat\rho$, $\widehat\eta$, $\nabla_{\widehat{x}}\widehat\eta$, $\partial_{s}\widehat\eta$ and $\partial_{a}\widehat\eta$ vanish, $\partial_{b}\widehat\rho = \frac1\mu \widehat{b}^* =: \widehat{h}^*$, and

$$\begin{aligned} |T||\omega| &= \int_{\widehat{\Omega}}\Big(\widehat\rho - \widehat{h}\cdot \widehat{b}\Big)\mathbb{I}:\nabla_{\widehat{x}} \widehat{v}_\omega(\widehat{x}) + \int_{\widehat{\Omega}} (\widehat{b}\otimes \widehat{h}):\nabla_{\widehat{x}} \widehat{v}_\omega(\widehat{x})\end{aligned}$$

which is the "standard" stress tensor we know. If, for example, $\mu=\mu(\widehat{x})$, then we additionally get

$$\begin{aligned} |T||\omega| \mathrel{{+}{=}} \int_{\widehat{\Omega}} \nabla_x\widehat\rho\cdot v\end{aligned}$$

and so on …

Appendix: Jacobi’s formula

Introduce the directional derivative

$$\begin{aligned} \nabla_T \det(A):=\lim_\lambda \frac{1}{\lambda}\Big(\det(A+\lambda T)-\det(A)\Big) \end{aligned}$$

Clearly, by division, we have $\nabla_T\det(A) = \det(A)\nabla_{TA^{-1}}\det(AA^{-1}) = \det(A)\nabla_{TA^{-1}}\det(\mathbb{I})$. Also, one can verify by Taylor expansion that

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}\det(A(s)) = \partial_{s}\det(A(s))\partial_{s}A(s) = \nabla_{\partial_{s}A(s)}\det(A(s))\end{aligned}$$

Moreover, by observing how the terms in the determinant formula group, we see that

$$\begin{aligned} \det(\mathbb{I}+\lambda T) = \prod_i (1+\lambda\cdot T_{ii}) + O(\lambda^2) =1+\lambda\cdot\text{tr}(T) + O(\lambda^2)\end{aligned}$$

from which we infer that $\nabla_T\det(\mathbb{I}) = \text{tr}(T)$, again, by employing the Taylor expansion. Thus

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}\det(A(s)) &= \nabla_{\partial_{s}A(s)}\det(A(s)) = \det(A(s))\nabla_{\partial_{s}A(s)A^{-1}(s)}\det(\mathbb{I}) \\ &=\det(A(s))\text{tr}\Big({\partial_{s}A(s)A^{-1}(s)}\Big)\end{aligned}$$

In our case, where $\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) = \det A(s)$ and $A(s)=\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})$, we have

$$\begin{aligned} \text{tr}\Big({\partial_{s}A(s)A^{-1}(s)}\Big) = \text{tr}\Big({\partial_{s}\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})\widehat{F}(s,\widehat{x})^{-1}}\Big) = \text{tr}\Big(D_x v(s,x)\Big) = \text{div}_x v(s,x)\end{aligned}$$

from which we ultimately derive that

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{ds}\widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x}) = \widehat{J}(s,\widehat{x})\cdot \text{div}_x v(s,x)\end{aligned}$$
